We achieve a "signature" look by leveraging a **Deconstructed Grid**. While the underlying structure is rigid, we break it through intentional "errors": baseline shifts in headers, overlapping containers, and asymmetrical color accents. The goal is to make the digital screen feel like a physical desk covered in rare, high-quality paper and ink-heavy documents. We are moving away from "UI components" and toward "information artifacts."

---

## 2. Colors & Surface Logic

The palette is rooted in the tactile world. The background is not white; it is a warm, aged paper. The primary "ink" is a deep charcoal.

<RuleBlock title="The 'No-Line' Rule" type="rule">
  **Prohibit 1px solid borders for sectioning.** To define a new content area, use a tonal shift. Use <code>surface_container_low</code> for a subtle recessed feel or <code>surface_container_highest</code> to suggest a thicker sheet of paper stacked on top. Boundaries are felt through value changes, not drawn with lines.
</RuleBlock>

### Glass & Texture
For floating elements or "top-secret" overlays, use **Glassmorphism**. Apply `surface_container_lowest` at 70% opacity with a `24px` backdrop-blur. This simulates a semi-translucent vellum paper, allowing the underlying "dossier" content to bleed through.

## 4. Elevation & Depth: Tonal Layering

We do not use Material-style drop shadows. Depth is an environmental effect, not a light-source effect.

<RuleBlock title="The Layering Principle" type="rule">
  Stack <code>surface-container</code> tiers. A "Project Card" should be <code>surface_container_highest</code> sitting on a <code>surface</code> background. The contrast in warmth/value creates the lift.
</RuleBlock>

<RuleBlock title="Ambient Shadows" type="rule">
  If an element must "float" (like a modal), use a shadow with a <code>48px</code> blur, <code>0%</code> spread, and <code>6%</code> opacity using the <code>on_surface</code> color. It should feel like a soft glow of ink rather than a harsh shadow.
</RuleBlock>

<RuleBlock title="The 'Ghost Border' Fallback" type="rule">
  If a boundary is visually required for accessibility, use the <code>outline_variant</code> token at <strong>15% opacity</strong>. It should be barely perceptible—a "hint" of an edge.
</RuleBlock>

## 6. Do’s and Don’ts

<RuleBlock title="Do: Use 'Ink Bleed' Accents" type="do">
  Use the cobalt or red accents as small, asymmetrical blocks of color that "bleed" off the edge of the screen.
</RuleBlock>

<RuleBlock title="Do: Embrace White Space" type="do">
  Use the <code>20</code> (7rem) and <code>24</code> (8.5rem) spacing tokens generously. Sophistication comes from the "void."
</RuleBlock>

<RuleBlock title="Do: Mix Case" type="do">
  Use <code>label-md</code> in all-caps for technical data, but keep <code>title-lg</code> in sentence case for a high-end editorial feel.
</RuleBlock>

<RuleBlock title="Don't: No Rounded Corners" type="dont">
  <code>0px</code> is the absolute rule. Any radius breaks the "raw" aesthetic.
</RuleBlock>

<RuleBlock title="Don't: No Standard Grids" type="dont">
  Avoid centering everything. Align text to the left but place secondary imagery or metadata on the far right of the grid to create tension.
</RuleBlock>

<RuleBlock title="Don't: No Cliches" type="dont">
  Absolutely no green "code" fonts or terminal windows. The sophistication is in the result of the work, not the tools of the work.
</RuleBlock>

<RuleBlock title="Don't: No Purple" type="dont">
  Adhere strictly to the Cobalt/Red/Yellow/Green accent palette.
</RuleBlock>
